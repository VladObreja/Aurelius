Biophysical Modalities in the Restoration of Vascular and Erectile Function: A Comprehensive Analysis of Acoustic and Electromagnetic Therapies
1. Introduction: The Paradigm Shift in Sexual Medicine and Circulatory Rehabilitation
The management of erectile dysfunction (ED) and peripheral circulatory disorders is currently undergoing a profound transformation. For the better part of three decades, the clinical approach to these conditions has been dominated by a palliative paradigm. The introduction of phosphodiesterase type 5 inhibitors (PDE5is) in the late 1990s revolutionized the field by providing effective symptomatic relief for millions of men. However, this pharmacological approach, while successful in temporarily restoring function, fails to address the underlying pathophysiology of the disease. It manages the symptom—the inability to attain an erection—without arresting or reversing the progressive vascular and cavernosal deterioration that defines the condition. Consequently, a significant cohort of patients, particularly those with severe vasculogenic pathology or post-surgical neurovascular injury, remain refractory to oral medication or become non-responders over time.
This limitation has necessitated a pivot toward "restorative therapies"—interventions designed not merely to mask symptoms but to rehabilitate the structural and functional integrity of the end-organ. This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the two most prominent biophysical modalities leading this charge: Acoustic Wave Therapy (specifically Low-Intensity Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy, or Li-ESWT) and Electromagnetic Stimulation (encompassing Pulsed Electromagnetic Field therapy [PEMF] and High-Intensity Focused Electromagnetic [HIFEM] technology).
We will explore the biophysical mechanisms that underpin these therapies, ranging from mechanotransduction and shear stress to ionic depolarization and neuromuscular re-education. The scope of this analysis extends beyond erectile dysfunction to include broader applications in circulation recovery, such as the treatment of peripheral artery disease (PAD) and diabetic angiopathy, recognizing that erectile dysfunction is often the sentinel manifestation of systemic vascular insufficiency. By synthesizing data from recent systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials (RCTs), and global clinical guidelines, this report aims to provide a definitive reference for the integration of these technologies into modern clinical practice.
1.1 The Pathophysiological Imperative
Erectile dysfunction is defined as the consistent or recurrent inability to attain and maintain an erection sufficient for satisfactory sexual performance. The prevalence is staggering, affecting approximately 52% of men between the ages of 40 and 70, with global projections estimating a burden of 322 million men by 2025. The etiology is multifactorial, categorized broadly into organic (vasculogenic, neurogenic, hormonal, anatomical) and psychogenic causes, though clinical reality often presents a mixed picture.
Vasculogenic ED, the most common phenotype, is inextricably linked to systemic cardiovascular health. It shares identical risk factors with coronary artery disease (CAD), including hypertension, diabetes mellitus, dyslipidemia, smoking, and obesity. The underlying pathology is endothelial dysfunction: an impairment in the bioavailability of nitric oxide (NO), which is essential for the relaxation of cavernosal smooth muscle. Over time, chronic ischemia leads to the apoptosis of smooth muscle cells and their replacement by collagenous fibrosis, resulting in a loss of veno-occlusive function (venous leak).
A distinct and clinically challenging subset involves iatrogenic injury following radical prostatectomy (RP). Despite advances in nerve-sparing robotic surgery, cavernous nerve neuropraxia is common, leading to a temporary or permanent cessation of nocturnal erections. This absence of nocturnal tumescence deprives the cavernosal tissue of oxygenated blood, accelerating a cycle of hypoxia, apoptosis, and fibrosis that renders the tissue unresponsive to future rehabilitation.
1.2 Limitations of the Current Standard of Care
The current standard of care—PDE5is (e.g., sildenafil, tadalafil), intracavernosal injections (ICI), and vacuum erection devices (VED)—relies on the presence of residual functional tissue. PDE5is require an intact nitric oxide pathway to function; they inhibit the degradation of cGMP but cannot generate it. In patients with severe endothelial damage or nerve injury, the substrate for these drugs is missing. Approximately 35% of patients do not respond to oral therapy. Furthermore, the "on-demand" nature of these treatments eliminates spontaneity, contributing to high discontinuation rates even among responders.
The promise of energy-based restorative therapies lies in their potential to induce endogenous tissue regeneration. By leveraging the principles of physics—specifically acoustic and electromagnetic energy—these modalities aim to stimulate angiogenesis (the formation of new blood vessels), recruit stem cells, and promote nerve regeneration, thereby restoring the patient's natural hemodynamic capacity.
2. Low-Intensity Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (Li-ESWT)
Low-Intensity Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (Li-ESWT) has emerged as the most investigated and clinically advanced restorative therapy for ED. Originally adapted from lithotripsy technology used to fragment kidney stones, Li-ESWT utilizes shock waves at significantly lower energy levels to stimulate tissue repair rather than destruction.
2.1 The Biophysics of Shock Waves
A shock wave is a distinct acoustic phenomenon characterized by specific physical parameters that differentiate it from standard sound waves or ultrasound. A true shock wave is defined by:
 * High Peak Pressure: Reaching up to 100 megapascals (MPa).
 * Rapid Rise Time: A virtually instantaneous rise in pressure, typically occurring in less than 10 nanoseconds.
 * Short Lifecycle: A total duration of less than 10 microseconds.
 * Frequency Spectrum: A broad frequency spectrum compared to the monochromatic nature of ultrasound.
In the context of Li-ESWT for soft tissue, the Energy Flux Density (EFD)—the amount of energy delivered per unit area—is the critical parameter. For ED treatment, EFDs are typically set between 0.05 and 0.09 mJ/mm². This range is sufficient to induce mechanical stress on the cellular level without causing thermal injury or cavitation-induced tissue damage.
2.2 Mechanism of Action: Mechanotransduction
The therapeutic efficacy of Li-ESWT is mediated through mechanotransduction, a process by which mechanical forces are converted into biochemical signals within the cell. As the shock wave propagates through the tissue, it encounters interfaces of varying acoustic impedance. This interaction generates compressive and tensile stresses, as well as shear forces, on the cell membranes and the extracellular matrix.
2.2.1 Angiogenesis and Neovascularization
The primary objective of Li-ESWT in vasculogenic ED is the restoration of penile blood flow through angiogenesis. The mechanical shear stress exerted on endothelial cells triggers a specific intracellular cascade:
 * Up-regulation of Angiogenic Factors: The physical stimulus induces the expression of immediate-early genes and transcription factors. Key among these is Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF), the most potent driver of blood vessel formation. Studies have consistently demonstrated increased levels of VEGF and its receptor, Flt-1, in cavernosal tissue following shock wave exposure.
 * Nitric Oxide Synthase Activation: Shear stress enhances the expression and activity of endothelial Nitric Oxide Synthase (eNOS). This enzyme is responsible for the production of NO, which not only facilitates immediate vasodilation but also acts as a signaling molecule for long-term vascular remodeling.
 * Proliferation Markers: Treatment leads to an increase in Proliferating Cell Nuclear Antigen (PCNA), a marker of active cell division, indicating endothelial proliferation and the formation of new capillary sprouts.
2.2.2 Stem Cell Recruitment and Activation
A more recently elucidated mechanism involves the recruitment of endogenous progenitor cells. The microtrauma caused by the shock waves creates a controlled, localized inflammatory environment. This "constructive inflammation" releases chemoattractant factors, such as Stromal Cell-Derived Factor 1 (SDF-1), which mobilize mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) from the bone marrow and local niches.
 * Differentiation: Once recruited to the corpora cavernosa, these stem cells can differentiate into endothelial cells and smooth muscle cells. Research indicates that Li-ESWT can provoke penile stem/progenitor cells (PSPCs) to express alpha-smooth muscle actin (alpha-SMA), suggesting a differentiation pathway that replenishes the smooth muscle content lost to fibrosis. This regenerative capacity is fundamental to reversing the veno-occlusive dysfunction associated with severe ED.
2.2.3 Nerve Regeneration
While angiogenesis addresses the vascular component, Li-ESWT also exhibits neurotrophic properties essential for treating post-prostatectomy and diabetic neurogenic ED.
 * Schwann Cell Proliferation: The acoustic energy stimulates the proliferation of Schwann cells, the support cells responsible for the myelination and repair of peripheral nerves.
 * Neurotrophic Factors: Treatment has been shown to upregulate Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF) and Neurotrophin-3. In animal models of cavernous nerve injury, Li-ESWT accelerated the regeneration of nNOS-containing nerve fibers, which are critical for initiating the erectile response.
2.3 Modalities: The Focused (fSWT) vs. Radial (rESWT) Debate
A critical distinction in the field—and a source of significant confusion for patients and practitioners alike—is the difference between focused shock wave therapy (fSWT) and radial pressure wave therapy (rESWT). While both are marketed under the umbrella of "shock wave therapy," they are biophysically distinct entities with divergent clinical profiles.
2.3.1 Focused Shock Wave Therapy (fSWT)
fSWT devices generate shock waves using electrohydraulic, electromagnetic, or piezoelectric sources. The key feature is the use of an acoustic lens or parabolic reflector to focus the energy to a specific point deep within the body.
 * Penetration and Precision: The energy converges at a focal zone, allowing the practitioner to target deep anatomical structures—such as the crura of the penis (located under the perineum) and the deep cavernosal arteries—without dissipating significant energy at the skin surface.
 * Waveform: fSWT produces a true shock wave with the characteristic rapid rise time (<10 ns) and high peak pressure necessary for effective mechanotransduction at depth.
 * Clinical Evidence: The vast majority of foundational animal studies and successful RCTs demonstrating histological changes (angiogenesis, nerve regeneration) have utilized fSWT. A comprehensive 2025 meta-analysis comparing the two modalities found that fSWT demonstrated superior efficacy, with a standardized mean difference (SMD) of 0.45 (95% CI: 0.04–0.86; P < 0.005) over radial waves in improving erectile function scores.
2.3.2 Radial Pressure Wave Therapy (rESWT)
rESWT devices generate acoustic waves pneumatically. A projectile is accelerated by compressed air within a handpiece, striking a metal applicator tip. The energy generated by this collision propagates radially—diverging from the point of contact like ripples in a pond.
 * Superficial Action: The maximum energy density is at the skin surface and attenuates rapidly as it travels deeper. The effective therapeutic depth is typically limited to 3–4 cm. While sufficient for superficial musculoskeletal conditions (e.g., plantar fasciitis, lateral epicondylitis), this depth is often inadequate to treat the entirety of the corporal bodies, particularly in obese patients or when targeting the crura.
 * Wave Characteristics: rESWT generates a "pressure wave" rather than a true shock wave. It lacks the rapid rise time and high peak pressure of fSWT.
 * Commercial Landscape: Due to regulatory classifications (often Class I medical devices vs. Class II/III for fSWT), radial devices are widely available in non-medical settings (e.g., spas, chiropractic offices) and are marketed directly to consumers under brand names like "GAINSWave".
 * Efficacy Controversy: The clinical data for rESWT in ED is mixed. While some studies suggest benefits—potentially via superficial hyperemia or placebo effect—meta-analyses consistently rank it inferior to fSWT. The rapid attenuation of energy means that the critical deep vascular structures may not receive a therapeutic dose.

2.4 Clinical Evidence in Erectile Dysfunction
The clinical validation of Li-ESWT has been extensive, with dozens of RCTs and systematic reviews published in the last decade.
2.4.1 Vasculogenic Erectile Dysfunction
This is the primary indication for Li-ESWT. The goal is to treat the underlying vascular insufficiency.
 * Efficacy Metrics: Treatment success is typically measured using the International Index of Erectile Function (IIEF-5) and the Erection Hardness Score (EHS). Systematic reviews consistently show statistically significant improvements in these scores compared to sham therapy.
 * The "Salvage" Effect: A critical finding is the ability of Li-ESWT to convert PDE5i non-responders into responders. By improving the baseline vascular bed, the therapy restores the substrate upon which the medication acts. Studies indicate that over 50% of non-responders can be salvaged, regaining the ability to have penetrative intercourse with medication.
 * Durability: Long-term follow-up data is emerging. A cohort study followed patients for 30 months and found that nearly 40% of responders maintained their improvement without needing additional treatment. However, efficacy tends to wane after 24 months, suggesting that maintenance sessions ("tune-ups") may be necessary to preserve the angiogenic benefits.
2.4.2 Post-Prostatectomy Erectile Dysfunction
The application of Li-ESWT in post-RP patients targets the recovery of the neurovascular bundle.
 * Systematic Review Findings: A 2025 systematic review of 10 studies (760 patients) confirmed that Li-ESWT is safe and potentially effective for this challenging population. While the magnitude of improvement is generally lower than in pure vasculogenic ED (due to the severity of nerve damage), it is statistically significant compared to control groups.
 * The Importance of Timing: The concept of "penile rehabilitation" posits that early intervention prevents irreversible damage. Studies comparing early (pre-catheter removal or immediately post-op) vs. delayed Li-ESWT intervention suggest that early treatment yields better long-term preservation of sexual function.
 * Combination Protocols: The most robust results are seen when Li-ESWT is combined with PDE5is or VEDs, creating a multimodal rehabilitation strategy that addresses both the neural and vascular components of the injury.
2.4.3 Protocol Standardization
One of the historical criticisms of Li-ESWT research has been the heterogeneity of protocols. However, recent meta-analyses have begun to define the optimal therapeutic window.
 * Energy Density: 0.09 mJ/mm² appears to be the "sweet spot." Lower energies (e.g., 0.05) may be insufficient, while higher energies do not necessarily yield better results and may be uncomfortable.
 * Shock Count: A total of 3000 shocks per session is recommended to ensure adequate coverage of the entire organ (distal, mid, proximal shaft bilaterally, plus the crura).
 * Frequency: Twice weekly sessions for 3 to 6 weeks (6-12 sessions total) is the standard regimen.
 * Anatomical Coverage: Treating the crura (the roots of the penis) is essential. The crura contain a significant volume of erectile tissue and the entry points of the cavernosal arteries. Failure to treat the crura is a common cause of treatment failure in radial wave protocols, which often cannot reach this depth.
3. Circulation Recovery: Systemic and Peripheral Applications
While the primary focus of this report is ED, the mechanisms of Li-ESWT and PEMF are applicable to broader circulatory recovery. The physiological principles of angiogenesis and microvascular repair are conserved across tissue types.
3.1 Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD) and Diabetic Angiopathy
Peripheral Artery Disease is the systemic analog to vasculogenic ED. It involves the narrowing of arteries in the limbs, leading to claudication (pain) and tissue ischemia.
 * Li-ESWT for PAD: Clinical trials have demonstrated that shock wave therapy can improve pain-free walking distance and maximum walking distance in PAD patients. The mechanism is identical to that in the penis: the induction of collateral vessel formation (arteriogenesis) to bypass occluded segments.
 * Diabetic Foot Ulcers (DFU): Both Li-ESWT and PEMF have shown efficacy in the healing of chronic diabetic foot ulcers.
   * Mechanism: In DFU, microcirculation is impaired due to endothelial dysfunction and basement membrane thickening. PEMF therapy has been shown to increase capillary blood flow and accelerate wound closure by promoting fibroblast proliferation and collagen synthesis.
   * Clinical Data: Studies indicate that PEMF-treated ulcers show improved microcirculation and significantly shorter healing times compared to standard care alone. This validates the angiogenic potential of these modalities in a highly compromised vascular environment, reinforcing their biological plausibility for treating diabetic ED.
4. Electromagnetic Stimulation Therapies
Electromagnetic therapies utilize magnetic fields to induce electrical currents or stimulate tissue, operating on different biophysical principles than acoustic waves.
4.1 Pulsed Electromagnetic Field (PEMF) Therapy
4.1.1 Mechanism: The Electrochemical Trigger
PEMF therapy does not rely on mechanical force. Instead, it utilizes low-frequency pulsed magnetic fields (typically 1–50 Hz) to induce weak electrical currents within the tissue, a phenomenon governed by Faraday's Law of Induction.
 * Calcium Channel Modulation: The induced currents affect the permeability of voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCC). This leads to an influx of intracellular calcium, which binds to calmodulin.
 * The NO Pathway: The Calcium-Calmodulin complex activates constitutive nitric oxide synthase (cNOS), leading to the immediate production of Nitric Oxide (NO). NO is the potent vasodilator responsible for erection initiation and maintenance.
 * Anti-Inflammatory Action: PEMF has a well-documented anti-inflammatory effect. It modulates cytokine profiles, reducing the chronic low-grade inflammation (endothelialitis) that characterizes diabetic vascular disease. By reducing inflammation, PEMF helps to restore normal endothelial function.
4.1.2 Clinical Utility in ED
While less studied than Li-ESWT, PEMF is gaining traction as a non-invasive adjunct.
 * Microcirculation: Given its proven efficacy in diabetic ulcers, PEMF is particularly suited for diabetic ED patients with severe microangiopathy.
 * Safety and Ease of Use: PEMF is painless, contactless, and can often be administered via home-use mats or localized applicators. It has no known significant side effects.
 * Nerve Regeneration: Preclinical studies suggest PEMF can accelerate peripheral nerve regeneration, making it a potential candidate for post-prostatectomy rehabilitation, potentially in combination with Li-ESWT.
4.2 High-Intensity Focused Electromagnetic (HIFEM) Technology
HIFEM represents a high-power application of magnetic stimulation, distinct from the low-power PEMF. Technologies like the "Emsella" chair utilize this modality.
4.2.1 Mechanism: Supramaximal Muscle Contraction
HIFEM generates a focused magnetic field of high intensity (up to 2.5 Tesla) that penetrates deep into the pelvic floor (up to 10 cm).
 * Motor Neuron Depolarization: The field induces electrical currents strong enough to depolarize motor neurons controlling the pelvic floor muscles (bulbospongiosus and ischiocavernosus).
 * Supramaximal Contractions: The stimulation overrides voluntary control, inducing thousands of "supramaximal" contractions in a single session. These contractions are far more intense than what can be achieved through voluntary Kegel exercises.
 * Physiological Impact: The bulbospongiosus muscle is responsible for penile engorgement during erection and the force of ejaculation. The ischiocavernosus muscle is critical for maintaining rigidity by compressing the venous outflow (preventing venous leak). Strengthening these muscles directly improves the hemodynamic mechanics of erection.
4.2.2 Clinical Evidence
 * Study Data: A pilot study of 28 men demonstrated that HIFEM treatment led to significant improvements in IIEF scores, specifically in the domains of erectile function and intercourse satisfaction.
 * Secondary Benefits: Patients also reported improved urinary control and ejaculatory strength.
 * Positioning: HIFEM is not primarily an angiogenic therapy; it is a neuromuscular rehabilitation therapy. It is best suited for patients with pelvic floor weakness, venous leak, or mixed urinary/sexual dysfunction.
### 4.3 Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS)
TENS involves the application of electrical current via skin electrodes.
 * Pudendal Nerve Stimulation: Direct stimulation of the pudendal nerve (which innervates the penis) can enhance sensation and reflex erectile activity.
 * Limitations: TENS is limited by the resistance of the skin and subcutaneous fat. It cannot easily reach the deep pelvic muscles or cavernous nerves without using invasive needle electrodes or uncomfortable intensities. However, it remains a low-cost, accessible option for home-based sensory rehabilitation.

5.1 The Power of Combination: Multimodal Protocols
Given the multifactorial nature of ED—often involving vascular insufficiency, smooth muscle atrophy, and nerve damage—combination therapies are emerging as the gold standard.
5.1.1 Li-ESWT + Vacuum Erection Device (VED)
 * Synergy: VEDs use negative pressure to mechanically distend the corpora cavernosa and draw arterial blood into the penis. This improves oxygenation and mechanically stretches the tissue.
 * Evidence: RCTs have shown that combining Li-ESWT with a specific VED protocol (e.g., 10 minutes daily) yields superior outcomes compared to either therapy alone. The VED may act as a form of "physical therapy," maximizing the angiogenic response triggered by the shock waves.
 * Protocol: Daily VED use prevents penile shortening (common after prostatectomy) and ensures that the newly forming vessels are subjected to functional demand.
5.1.2 Li-ESWT + Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)
 * Regenerative Amplification: PRP is a concentration of autologous growth factors derived from the patient's blood. When injected into the corpora, it provides a biochemical "fertilizer" for the stem cells recruited by Li-ESWT.
 * Clinical Data: Preliminary studies indicate that the combination of Li-ESWT and PRP results in a greater increase in IIEF scores (mean difference of 8.2 points) compared to Li-ESWT alone. This combination represents a potent "biologic + biophysical" approach to tissue engineering in vivo.
5.1.3 Li-ESWT + PDE5 Inhibitors
 * The "Salvage" Protocol: For non-responders, the concurrent use of daily tadalafil (5mg) during the Li-ESWT treatment course is standard. The medication maintains a background level of vasodilation, which may enhance the transport of angiogenic factors and stem cells mobilized by the shock waves.
6. Guidelines, Regulatory Landscape, and Socioeconomics
6.1 The Discrepancy in Global Guidelines
There is a notable divergence in how major urological societies view Li-ESWT.
 * European Association of Urology (EAU): In its 2025 guidelines, the EAU explicitly recommends Li-ESWT as a treatment option for patients with vasculogenic ED who desire a restorative approach or who are poor responders to PDE5is. The EAU acknowledges the strength of the meta-analytic evidence, particularly for focused shock waves.
 * Sexual Medicine Society of North America (SMSNA) & American Urological Association (AUA): These bodies have historically classified Li-ESWT as "investigational" (Grade C evidence). Their hesitation stems from the heterogeneity of past studies (mixing radial and focused data) and the lack of a single, standardized, large-scale multicenter RCT in the US. However, they acknowledge the safety of the modality and encourage its study.
 * Implication: This divergence affects insurance coverage. In Europe, coverage is becoming more common; in the US, it remains largely a cash-pay procedure.
6.2 FDA Status and Consumer Safety
 * FDA Approval: As of 2025, no shock wave device has specific FDA approval for the indication of "Erectile Dysfunction". Devices like the Storz Duolith are FDA-cleared for musculoskeletal indications (Class II) and are used "off-label" by physicians for ED.
 * The Radial Wave Market: The market is flooded with Class I radial devices (e.g., The Phoenix, various "acoustic wave" clinic franchises) marketed directly to consumers. These devices are safe but, as discussed, biophysically inferior to focused shock waves for treating deep erectile tissue. Patients must be educated that "acoustic wave therapy" at a spa is likely not the same as the "shock wave therapy" described in medical literature.
6.3 Socioeconomic Considerations
The cost of a full course of clinical Li-ESWT (typically 6-12 sessions) ranges from $3,000 to $6,000 in the US. Since it is not covered by insurance, access is limited to those with disposable income. However, when compared to the lifetime cost of PDE5is (which can be substantial) and the psychological "cost" of ED, many patients view it as a worthwhile investment. The development of safe, effective, and regulated home-use devices could significantly democratize access to this care in the future.
7. Future Directions: The Horizon of Restorative Urology
The trajectory of sexual medicine is clear: a move away from lifelong symptom management toward curative, restorative interventions.
7.1 Personalized Medicine
Future protocols will likely be tailored to the patient's specific vascular and neural profile.
 * Diagnostic Integration: Utilizing Penile Doppler Ultrasound to stratify patients. Patients with pure arterial insufficiency may receive Li-ESWT; those with venous leak may receive a combination of HIFEM and Li-ESWT.
 * Genomic Markers: Understanding genetic variations in VEGF or eNOS expression could help predict "super-responders" to shock wave therapy.
7.2 Technological Evolution
 * Home-Based Devices: The miniaturization of technology is leading to the development of home-use devices. While current iterations (mostly radial) are limited, future "prescribable" home fSWT units with safety locks and verified energy outputs could transform the delivery of care.
 * Stem Cell Integration: The combination of Li-ESWT with autologous stem cell therapy (e.g., stromal vascular fraction) is the next frontier, potentially offering a cure for even severe, post-surgical ED cases.
7.3 Conclusion
The evidence base for Low-Intensity Extracorporeal Shock Wave Therapy (Li-ESWT) has matured significantly. It is a safe, effective, and biologically plausible treatment for vasculogenic and post-prostatectomy ED, capable of inducing genuine tissue regeneration. Electromagnetic therapies (PEMF/HIFEM) offer complementary benefits, particularly for microvascular support and pelvic floor rehabilitation.
For the clinician, the integration of these modalities represents a powerful expansion of the therapeutic armamentarium. For the patient, they offer something that pills never could: the possibility of reversing the disease process and restoring natural, spontaneous function. As research continues to refine protocols and devices, biophysical energy will undoubtedly become a cornerstone of modern regenerative urology.
